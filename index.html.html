LESSON 01 — INTRODUCTION

What Is the Electrical System?

The big picture before you learn the details. No experience needed.

The Big Idea

Welcome to the electrical subteam. If you've never touched a robot, never wired anything, and have zero electronics experience — this is exactly the right place to start. We're going to build your knowledge from the ground up, one layer at a time.

Let's start with the most important question: what does the electrical system actually do?

Think about a car. A car has an engine that makes power, a steering wheel that tells it where to go, sensors that warn you when something is wrong, and lights so you can see at night. Every single one of those things relies on electricity. The robot is the same way.

💡 ANALOGY

The electrical system is like the nervous system of a human body. Your brain sends signals through nerves to move your muscles. The robot's electrical system sends signals from a computer through wires to motors. Without it, the robot is just a pile of metal that can't do anything.

The electrical subteam's job is to safely power the robot, make sure all the components can talk to each other, and ensure the driver can control everything from across the field.

The Three Jobs of Electrical

Everything the electrical subteam does falls into one of these three jobs:

1. Power Distribution

Getting the right amount of electricity from the battery to every component that needs it. This is like plumbing — you need the right pipe sizes, and water (electricity) needs to reach every room (component).

2. Communication

Making sure the robot's brain (computer) can talk to the motors, sensors, and everything else. Without communication, the brain can't tell the motors to spin, and the sensors can't tell the brain what they detect.

3. Control

Connecting the driver station (the laptop at the field) to the robot so the human driver's joystick commands actually move the robot. This involves the radio, which is like a Wi-Fi router for the robot.

LESSON 02 — POWER SYSTEM

Power — Battery to Everything

How electricity travels from a single battery to every single component on the robot.

Start Simple: Power Is Like Water

Before we talk about components, let's understand electricity the easy way.

💡 ANALOGY — WATER IN PIPES

Think of electricity like water flowing through pipes. The battery is the water tower — it holds pressure (voltage). The wires are pipes. Components are faucets and appliances that need water to work. More water pressure = more power available. If a pipe is too narrow (too thin a wire), it gets hot and can break. If you open too many faucets at once, pressure drops and things stop working — this is called a brownout on an FRC robot.

On a real FRC robot, the "water pressure" is 12 volts — that's the voltage the battery provides. Everything on the robot runs off that 12V, though some devices need it stepped down to lower voltages (we'll cover that when we get to the C Buck Converter in Lesson 7).

The Battery

WE USE THIS

The battery is the only source of power on the robot. No battery = no robot. It provides 12 volts of direct current (DC) — similar to a car battery, but specifically sized for FRC.

The Generic FRC Battery

Most FRC teams use a 12V, 18Ah sealed lead-acid battery. The "18Ah" means it can deliver 18 amps of current for 1 hour, or 36 amps for 30 minutes, before it goes dead. In a match, a robot draws a lot of power — especially when driving and running mechanisms at the same time.

The Energizer FRC Battery

Some teams, including ours, use the Energizer EV12FR (or similar Energizer FRC-spec battery). This battery is specifically designed for high-current discharge, meaning it holds up better during intense match play. It has lower internal resistance, so it delivers more stable voltage even under heavy load — reducing brownouts. Think of it as a "premium water tower" that maintains pressure even when all the faucets are open.

The Main Breaker

WE USE THIS

The main breaker is a large red switch at the top of the robot. It is the master on/off switch for ALL electrical power on the robot. When you flip it off, every component instantly loses power.

💡 ANALOGY

The main breaker is like the main circuit breaker panel in a house. There's one big switch at the top that controls everything. If you flip that one switch, every light, outlet, and appliance in the house goes dead. On the robot, the main breaker does the same thing — one switch kills all power instantly.

The main breaker also serves as a 120A (amp) overcurrent protector. If something catastrophically wrong happens and too much current flows through the system, it trips (pops) to prevent fires and damage. Think of it as a safety valve.

💚 RULE

In FRC competition, the main breaker must be accessible from outside the robot. Inspectors will check this. The robot must be able to be shut off immediately by hitting the main breaker — this is a safety requirement.

The Power Distribution Hub (PDH)

WE USE THIS — IN DEPTH IN COMPONENT LIBRARY
See full PDH deep-dive in the Components section →

After the main breaker, power goes straight to the PDH — Power Distribution Hub. The PDH is like the fuse box in a house. Where a house's fuse box has individual circuit breakers for each room, the PDH has individual fuse-protected channels for each component on the robot.

The PDH has 20 high-current channels (for motors) and 4 low-current channels (for sensors and small devices). Each channel is individually fused so that if one component short-circuits, it doesn't take down the whole robot.

💚 KEY INSIGHT

Every single component on the robot gets its power from the PDH, directly or indirectly. The PDH is the hub of all power — that's why its failure is catastrophic. Always make sure PDH connections are tight and fuses are correct before powering on.

Brownouts — When Too Much Power Is Drawn

A brownout happens when the robot draws more current than the battery can cleanly supply. The voltage drops below 6.8V and the roboRIO (the brain — we'll meet it properly in Lesson 3) shuts down non-essential outputs to protect itself. This causes the robot to momentarily go limp or behave strangely.

⚠️ WATCH OUT

Brownouts most commonly happen when all four drive motors try to accelerate hard at the same time, while the battery is already partially drained. Good programming can help by ramping motor speeds gradually. On the electrical side, using quality batteries and keeping connections clean reduces brownout risk.

🔧 MENTOR NOTE

Advanced note: The PDH connects to the roboRIO via CAN Bus for power monitoring. This allows the code to read per-channel current draw in real time — useful for detecting stalls (a motor trying to spin but being physically blocked). Cover this connection in Lesson 4 on CAN Bus.

LESSON 03 — THE BRAIN

The roboRIO 2.0

The robot's main computer — everything else exists to serve it or be controlled by it.

Reminder from Lesson 2: The roboRIO gets its power from the PDH — the fuse box we learned about. →

What Is the roboRIO?

💡 ANALOGY — THE BRAIN

The roboRIO is the brain of the robot. Just like your brain receives signals from your senses and sends commands to your muscles, the roboRIO receives sensor data and driver commands, runs the robot's code, and sends control signals to the motors and other actuators. Without a brain, your body can't do anything. Without the roboRIO, the robot is just metal and motors that do nothing.

The roboRIO 2.0 is manufactured by National Instruments (NI) and is the only approved robot controller for FRC competition. You cannot use any other computer as the primary controller — this is a rule.

It runs a real-time operating system and executes the robot code that your programming subteam writes (usually in Java, C++, or Python). Every decision the robot makes — turn left, fire a shooter, raise an arm — comes from code running on the roboRIO.

roboRIO 1.0 vs 2.0

The roboRIO 2.0 was released in 2022. If you ever see an older roboRIO, you can tell the difference: the 2.0 has a USB-C port and 3x more memory. The 2.0 loads code much faster and is more reliable at high-speed CAN communication — which matters when you have many motors on CAN Bus (we'll cover CAN Bus in Lesson 4).

💚 ALWAYS USE THE 2.0

If your team has both versions, always use the 2.0 for competition robots. The 1.0 is fine for practice bots but can struggle with heavy CAN loads.

The Robot Signal Light (RSL)

Connected directly to the roboRIO is the RSL — Robot Signal Light. This is the orange blinking/solid light you see on competition robots. It communicates the robot's status:

The RSL is an FRC safety requirement. A robot without a working RSL will not pass inspection. Make sure it is mounted visibly on the outside of the robot and wired correctly. If the RSL is not working, check that it's connected to the RSL port on the roboRIO and that the roboRIO has power.

The System Core

WE USE THIS

The System Core (by Cross The Road Electronics / CTRE) is a newer component that works alongside the roboRIO. Think of it as a co-processor — a second, smaller brain that handles specific high-speed tasks so the roboRIO doesn't get overwhelmed.

💡 ANALOGY

Imagine the roboRIO is the principal of a school. The System Core is the vice principal who handles all the day-to-day paperwork so the principal can focus on the big decisions. Together, they're more efficient than the principal trying to do everything alone.

The System Core connects to the roboRIO via CAN Bus (which we'll learn in Lesson 4) and handles high-frequency motor control data. It lets the robot run more motors at higher speeds with less latency.

🔧 MENTOR NOTE

Advanced: The roboRIO runs NI's real-time OS (Linux-based). The robot code runs in user space with WPILib handling hardware abstraction. For teams using Phoenix 6 (CTRE's framework), the CANivore (covered in Lesson 4) offloads CAN Bus management entirely, reducing roboRIO CPU load significantly.

LESSON 04 — COMMUNICATION

CAN Bus — The Nervous System

How every component on the robot talks to the brain.

Remember from Lesson 3: The roboRIO is the brain. CAN Bus is how it talks to everything else. →

What Is CAN Bus?

💡 ANALOGY — THE INTERCOM SYSTEM

Imagine a large office building with one intercom system that connects every room. Instead of calling each room one-by-one on separate phone lines, the boss (roboRIO) broadcasts on the shared intercom. Every room (motor controller, sensor) hears the broadcast, but only the ones whose number is called respond. This shared wire network is CAN Bus. It's one wire loop that all devices share, and each device has a unique "address" (called a CAN ID) so messages get to the right place.

CAN stands for Controller Area Network. It was originally designed for cars — every modern vehicle uses CAN Bus so the engine computer can talk to the transmission, the brakes, the dashboard, and sensors, all on just two wires. FRC robots use the same technology.

The two CAN wires are usually yellow (CAN High) and green (CAN Low). They form a loop (a daisy chain or ring) connecting all CAN devices together. At each end of the chain, there are 120Ω termination resistors to prevent signal reflections (the PDH and the last device typically handle this).

What Devices Are on CAN Bus?

On our robot, the following devices communicate over CAN Bus:

  • roboRIO — the bus master / brain
  • PDH — power monitor, receives enable/disable commands
  • CANivore — a CAN Bus booster/router (covered below)
  • Kraken X44 / X60 motors — their built-in controllers communicate over CAN
  • Pigeon 2.0 — IMU/gyro, sends orientation data over CAN
  • CAN Range — distance sensor on CAN
  • CANANDCOLOR — color/proximity sensor on CAN
💚 CAN IDs

Every CAN device needs a unique ID number (0–62). If two devices share the same ID, they'll conflict and both will malfunction. IDs are set using configuration software (Phoenix Tuner X for CTRE devices, REV Hardware Client for REV devices). Always verify IDs before competition.

The CANivore

WE USE THIS
Remember from Lesson 3: The roboRIO (the brain) connects to the CANivore, which then manages a separate CAN Bus network. →
💡 ANALOGY — A DEDICATED EXPRESSWAY

Imagine traffic on a highway. Without the CANivore, all CAN Bus traffic has to go through the roboRIO's one lane — it gets congested with many fast cars (motors). The CANivore is like building a separate, faster expressway. It handles all the high-speed motor data on its own, letting the roboRIO's regular road stay clear for less urgent tasks.

The CANivore connects to the roboRIO via USB and creates a separate, isolated CAN Bus network running at 1 Mbit/s (vs the standard 1 Mbit/s shared bus). All CTRE devices (Kraken motors, Pigeon 2.0, etc.) can connect to the CANivore's bus.

Benefits of the CANivore:

  • Lower latency for motor control (critical for swerve drive)
  • Reduces roboRIO CPU load
  • Separates high-speed motor traffic from other CAN devices
  • Better diagnostics — you can see per-device status in Phoenix Tuner X

The Mini Power Module

WE USE THIS

The Mini Power Module (MPM) is a small CTRE device that provides clean, regulated 12V power to the CANivore and other small devices. It plugs into one of the PDH's 20A channels and outputs stable power.

💡 ANALOGY

Think of the Mini Power Module as a clean, dedicated wall outlet in a nice surge-protected power strip. Even when the main power in the house (PDH) is fluctuating from all the motors running, the MPM provides clean, stable power to the sensitive electronics connected to it.

The MPM also connects to CAN Bus so it can be monitored and it provides power to devices like the CANivore, which then manages the CAN Bus for the motors. In Lesson 7, we'll also see that the C Buck Converter draws from the MPM to power the Orange Pi 5.

Troubleshooting CAN Bus

CAN Bus problems are common and can be tricky for beginners. Here's what to look for:

🔧 MENTOR NOTE

The roboRIO's internal CAN Bus runs at 1 Mbit/s but with higher latency due to OS overhead. The CANivore bypasses this using a dedicated USB-CAN bridge with Phoenix's pro library, achieving 250Hz+ control loops for swerve. For teams using Phoenix 6 Pro, the CANivore is nearly mandatory for competitive swerve performance.

LESSON 05 — MOTORS

Motors & Movement

Kraken motors, MK5i swerve modules, and how robots actually drive.

Remember from Lesson 4: Kraken motors communicate over CAN Bus, managed by the CANivore. →

How Motors Work (The Simple Version)

💡 ANALOGY — ELECTRIC MOTORS

A motor is like a hand pushing a wheel. Plug it in, electricity flows through magnets inside, and the shaft spins. The faster you send electricity, the faster it spins. The harder the shaft is loaded (like trying to push through mud), the more current it draws — just like how you breathe harder when carrying heavy things. A motor controller (built into the Kraken) is the "throttle" that controls how much electricity flows to the motor.

FRC motors need a motor controller to operate. The motor controller takes a command from the roboRIO (via CAN Bus) and converts it into the right amount of power from the PDH to make the motor spin at the desired speed and direction.

Kraken X60 Motor

WE USE THIS

The Kraken X60 is a high-performance brushless motor by CTRE (Cross The Road Electronics) with a built-in motor controller (called TalonFX) integrated directly into the motor body. This means no separate motor controller box — the smart controller is inside the motor itself.

💡 ANALOGY — ALL-IN-ONE

Older FRC motors (like the CIM or Falcon 500) needed a separate controller box wired to them — like a TV needing a separate cable box. The Kraken X60 is like a modern smart TV — the "cable box" is built right in. Less wiring, less weight, more reliable.

Kraken X44 Motor

WE USE THIS

The Kraken X44 is a smaller, lighter version of the Kraken X60. It uses the same TalonFX controller and CAN Bus interface, but in a more compact package. It's used for mechanisms where space or weight is at a premium — like steering modules inside swerve pods where clearance is tight.

The wiring is identical to the X60 — power from PDH, CAN Bus to CANivore. The main differences are size, weight, and peak power output (slightly lower than the X60).

💚 X44 vs X60 — HOW TO CHOOSE

X60 for drive wheels (high power needed). X44 for steering (precision needed, less power). Both use the same code API since they're both TalonFX devices.

MK5i Swerve Modules

WE USE THIS

The MK5i is a swerve drive module made by Swerve Drive Specialties (SDS). Each swerve module is a complete wheel-and-steering assembly that can rotate the wheel 360° while also driving it. Four of these modules go in the corners of the robot to create a swerve drivetrain.

💡 ANALOGY — SHOPPING CART WHEELS

Imagine if all four wheels on a shopping cart could be pointed and driven in any direction at the same time, independently. That's swerve drive. A regular tank drive robot turns like a tank — pivot in place or arc. A swerve robot can move sideways, diagonally, spin while driving — all without changing direction of the robot body. It's the most maneuverable drivetrain in FRC.

Each MK5i module contains:

  • One Kraken X60 (drive motor) — spins the wheel to go forward/backward
  • One Kraken X44 (steer motor) — rotates the wheel to change direction
  • A CANCoder (absolute encoder) — tells the code exactly which angle the wheel is pointing

With 4 modules, that's 8 Kraken motors + 4 CANCoders on CAN Bus for the drive train alone. This is why the CANivore and its dedicated high-speed bus are so important for swerve.

⚠️ COMMON MISTAKE — SWERVE WHEEL ALIGNMENT

Before every match, or after any collision, verify that all four swerve wheels are pointing forward. Misaligned wheels at startup cause the robot to drive unpredictably. The absolute CANCoder encoders should read the correct zero positions — check these in Phoenix Tuner X.

🔧 MENTOR NOTE

The MK5i uses a 1:1 bevel gear and two-stage planetary reduction. Drive gear ratio options are L1, L2, L3, L4 — your team has chosen [EDIT: fill in your gear ratio]. Higher ratios = more speed, less torque. For Krakens, L3 or L4 with Kraken X60 is a common competition setup. Make sure students understand that the gear ratio affects both the free speed calculation and the physics simulation parameters in code.

LESSON 06 — SENSORS

Sensors & Feedback

How the robot senses the world — Pigeon 2.0, CAN Range, CANANDCOLOR, and more.

Remember from Lesson 4: Sensors communicate over CAN Bus, sending data back to the roboRIO (the brain) in real time. →

Why Do Robots Need Sensors?

💡 ANALOGY — YOUR BODY'S SENSES

Close your eyes and try to walk in a straight line. You'll drift. Now close your eyes and try to pick up an object without knowing where it is. Without your senses, your brain can't make accurate decisions. The robot is the same. Without sensors, the code is "flying blind" — it doesn't know if the robot is tilting, where it's facing, how far away a target is, or whether a mechanism is in the right position.

Pigeon 2.0 (IMU/Gyro)

WE USE THIS

The Pigeon 2.0 is an IMU — Inertial Measurement Unit. It measures the robot's orientation in 3D space: yaw (left/right rotation), pitch (front/back tilt), and roll (side tilt). It also measures acceleration.

💡 ANALOGY — THE INNER EAR

Your inner ear is your body's IMU. It tells your brain which way is up, whether you're tilting, and how fast you're moving. Without it, you'd fall over. The Pigeon 2.0 is the robot's inner ear — it's how the robot knows which direction it's facing (yaw/heading), which is critical for swerve drive field-oriented control. Without the Pigeon, the swerve drive would have no idea which way "forward" is relative to the field.

The Pigeon 2.0 connects to CAN Bus (on the CANivore network) and sends heading and acceleration data hundreds of times per second. It's used by:

  • Swerve drive for field-oriented control (knowing where "field-forward" is)
  • Auto-balance routines (detecting tilt)
  • Odometry (tracking the robot's position on the field)

💚 MOUNTING TIP

Mount the Pigeon 2.0 as close to the center of the robot as possible, and as low as possible. The closer to center, the less it gets confused by the robot's rotational inertia. Never mount it on a piece of metal that vibrates a lot — it will introduce noise into the gyro readings.

CAN Range

SITUATIONAL — NOT ON EVERY ROBOT

The CAN Range is a CTRE distance sensor that communicates over CAN Bus. It uses Time-of-Flight (ToF) technology — it sends out an invisible light pulse and measures how long it takes to bounce back — to measure distance to an object.

⚠️ WHEN TO USE CAN RANGE

CAN Range is only used on robots that genuinely need precise distance sensing — for example, detecting exactly when a game piece has entered an intake, or measuring gap distance for a precise mechanism. It should NOT be added "just in case." Every CAN device adds to bus utilization and complexity. If a simpler sensor (like a beam break or limit switch) will do the job, use that instead.

Range: up to ~2 meters, with millimeter accuracy. It connects via CAN Bus and mounts directly to the robot using the mounting holes on the sensor body. Because it's CAN-based, it reports distance directly to the roboRIO (through the CANivore) without needing additional wiring to a DIO port.

CANANDCOLOR by Redux Robotics

SITUATIONAL — VERY IMPORTANT TO UNDERSTAND

The CANANDCOLOR is made by Redux Robotics. It's a multi-function CAN sensor that can detect color, proximity, and through-beam (game piece detection) — all over CAN Bus.

💡 ANALOGY

Think of the CANANDCOLOR as a sensor that can see both what color something is and whether something is right in front of it. Imagine a robotic arm that needs to know (1) whether it's holding a note vs. a ring, and (2) whether the note is fully seated. One CANANDCOLOR sensor can answer both questions.

Common uses in FRC:

  • Detecting whether an intake has captured a game piece (proximity mode)
  • Differentiating between game piece types (color mode)
  • Confirming a ball or ring is seated in a mechanism before shooting

The CANANDCOLOR is situational — not every game requires color detection. But when the game involves multiple types of game pieces, or when reliable game-piece detection is critical, this sensor is extremely valuable. It avoids the need for separate analog/DIO sensor wiring by keeping everything on CAN.

🔧 MENTOR NOTE

The Pigeon 2.0 has a known drift issue when placed in high-vibration environments — using software fusion with vision data (PhotonVision / April Tags) greatly reduces heading error over long matches. Ensure the Phoenix 6 firmware is up to date on the Pigeon. For CANANDCOLOR, check the Redux Robotics documentation for their vendor library (not included in WPILib by default).

LESSON 07 — VISION & COMMUNICATION

Vision & Communication

Orange Pi 5, cameras, radio, Ethernet, and the C Buck Converter — the robot's eyes and voice.

Remember from Lesson 3: The roboRIO receives driver commands via the Radio through Ethernet. Now we'll explain how the whole communication chain works. →

The Radio (by Vivid-Hosting)

WE USE THIS
💡 ANALOGY — THE WI-FI ROUTER

At home, your Wi-Fi router connects to the internet (the FMS / Field Management System) and broadcasts a wireless signal to your devices (driver station laptop and the robot's roboRIO). Without it, your devices can't communicate. The Robot Radio does exactly this — it wirelessly bridges the driver station on the field to the robot.

The radio (by Vivid-Hosting) is the FRC-approved 802.11 radio used since the 2024 season. It connects to:

  • Ethernet port #1 → connects to the roboRIO
  • Ethernet port #2 → connects to the Orange Pi 5
  • Power → from the Mini Power Module's regulated output (or similar 12V regulated source)

The radio receives wireless commands from the driver station (running the FRC Driver Station software) and forwards them to the roboRIO. It also passes field match data (autonomous period start, enable/disable signals) from the FMS at competitions.

⚠️ CRITICAL — RADIO CONFIGURATION

The radio must be configured (flashed) with the correct team number using the FRC Radio Configuration Utility before each competition. An unconfigured radio will not connect to the Field Management System. This is one of the most common causes of robots being unable to connect at competition.

Ethernet on the Robot

WE USE THIS

Ethernet is the same kind of networking cable used in homes and offices — it's the standard for wired high-speed data. On the robot, Ethernet is used to connect devices that need high-bandwidth, reliable communication.

On our robot, Ethernet connects:

  • Radio → roboRIO (driver commands, FMS data)
  • Radio → Orange Pi 5 (vision data flows from the Pi to roboRIO via the radio's internal switch)
💚 ETHERNET CABLE TIPS

Use CAT5e or CAT6 Ethernet cables. Make sure connectors are fully clicked in — a half-seated Ethernet connector is one of the hardest intermittent problems to diagnose. If a robot behaves normally in the pits but fails on the field, check all Ethernet connections first.

Orange Pi 5 — The Vision Co-Processor

WE USE THIS
💡 ANALOGY — A DEDICATED VISION EXPERT

Imagine the roboRIO (the brain/principal) also had to personally watch every security camera in the building and call out the location of every visitor, while simultaneously running all the other school operations. It would get overwhelmed. So instead, we hire a specialist — the Orange Pi 5 — whose only job is to watch the cameras, identify AprilTags, calculate field positions, and report them to the principal. The principal (roboRIO) just hears the reports and acts on them, without doing any of the vision work itself.

The Orange Pi 5 is a single-board computer (like a Raspberry Pi, but more powerful) that runs vision software — typically PhotonVision or Limelight software. Its job is to:

  • Process camera frames looking for AprilTags (2D barcodes on the field)
  • Calculate the robot's pose (position + orientation) on the field using multi-camera tag detection
  • Send that pose data to the roboRIO via Ethernet (using PhotonVision's network tables)

The Orange Pi 5 connects to:

  • Ethernet → to the Radio (which bridges data to the roboRIO)
  • USB → cameras plugged directly in
  • Power → from the C Buck Converter

C Buck Converter

WE USE THIS
💡 ANALOGY — THE PHONE CHARGER

Your phone needs 5V. Your wall outlet provides 120V (AC). Your charger is a buck converter — it takes high voltage in, and outputs lower voltage out. The C Buck Converter on the robot takes the robot's 12V power and converts it to the correct voltage (typically USB-C at 5V or a specific barrel connector voltage) that the Orange Pi 5 needs. Without it, plugging the Pi directly into the robot's 12V supply would destroy it.

The complete power chain for the Orange Pi 5:

⚡ PDH (12V raw)
↓ via dedicated channel
🔌 Mini Power Module (clean regulated 12V)
↓ output wire to
🔄 C Buck Converter (converts 12V → correct Pi voltage, ~5V via USB-C or barrel)
↓ USB-C or barrel connector
🖥️ Orange Pi 5 (powered up and running vision)

Think of it like this: the Mini Power Module is a clean, dedicated wall outlet. The C Buck Converter is the adapter that makes sure the Orange Pi 5 gets exactly the right voltage from that outlet — just like how your phone charger converts the wall's 120V to the 5V your phone needs.

Cameras on the Robot

WE USE THIS

FRC robots use cameras for two purposes: vision processing (finding AprilTags, detecting game pieces) and driver view (seeing what the robot sees on the driver station display).

USB Cameras

Simple webcams or machine-vision cameras that plug into USB. They connect to the Orange Pi 5 (for vision processing) or directly to the roboRIO (for basic driver view streaming). USB cameras are simpler to set up but have limited resolution and frame rate.

IP Cameras / GigE Cameras

Higher-performance cameras that connect via Ethernet and have their own IP address on the network. They're used for higher-resolution vision at higher frame rates. They plug into the robot's Ethernet network (via the Radio's switch ports).

💚 CAMERA PLACEMENT

For AprilTag detection, cameras should be mounted as high as possible with a wide field of view, angled slightly downward. The further from center, the better the parallax for pose estimation. Use PhotonVision's camera calibration tool before every season — calibration is critical for accurate pose estimates.

🔧 MENTOR NOTE

The Orange Pi 5 runs Ubuntu 22.04 with PhotonVision installed as a service. Access the PhotonVision web dashboard at the Pi's IP (usually 10.TE.AM.11) to tune pipelines. Ensure the Pi's hostname is set correctly for NetworkTables connection to the roboRIO. The C Buck Converter used is typically a DROK or similar 12V-to-5V/9V USB-C PD converter — verify the Pi's power requirements (the Orange Pi 5 can pull ~10W at peak). Confirm the MPM channel fuse rating matches.

Power Distribution Hub (PDH)

CTRE — POWER DISTRIBUTION
CRITICAL COMPONENT

The PDH (Power Distribution Hub by CTRE) is the power manager of the entire robot. Every component that needs electricity gets it from the PDH — directly or indirectly. Think of it as the electrical panel in a house: it takes one big power source (the battery) and distributes it through individual protected circuits to everything that needs it.

What It Does

The PDH takes 12V power from the main breaker and routes it through 20 high-current channels (up to 40A each, protected by blade fuses) and 4 low-current "switchable" channels (up to 15A, software-controlled). It also connects to CAN Bus so the roboRIO can read real-time current draw from each channel — incredibly useful for detecting motor stalls or debugging power problems.

How to Connect It

Input: thick red (+) and black (−) cables from the main breaker output. Output: individual channels with fuses, each going to a motor controller or device. CAN: yellow/green CAN wires connecting to the rest of the CAN Bus chain.

⚠️ COMMON MISTAKES

1. Installing the wrong fuse size — always match the fuse to the wire gauge and device current rating, NOT the maximum current the device can draw. 2. Loose screws on PDH terminals — vibration can loosen them over a season, causing intermittent power loss. Check with a torque screwdriver before every competition.

roboRIO 2.0

NATIONAL INSTRUMENTS / NI — ROBOT CONTROLLER
CRITICAL COMPONENT

The roboRIO 2.0 is the mandatory FRC robot controller — the brain. It runs your team's robot code (Java/C++/Python via WPILib), manages all CAN Bus communication, interfaces with the radio, and provides digital/analog I/O for sensors. It receives 12V from the PDH and regulates it internally for safe logic-level operation.

Key ports: 1× USB-A (host), 1× USB-C (programming/config), 2× Ethernet (typically only 1 used — to radio), 10× DIO, 4× AIO, 1× SPI, 1× I2C, 1× RS-232, CAN Bus in/out, RSL output.

Components We Don't Use

You'll see these on other teams' robots or in older documentation. Know what they are.

These components are not on our current robot, but they're common in FRC and you should be able to recognize and understand them. Many teams still use them, and you'll see them at competitions.

SPARK MAX Motor Controller

REV ROBOTICS — BRUSHLESS/BRUSHED MOTOR CONTROLLER

The SPARK MAX is REV Robotics' motor controller — the competitor to CTRE's TalonFX. It controls brushless motors (like the REV NEO and NEO 550) or brushed motors. Unlike the Kraken, the SPARK MAX is a separate box that connects between the PDH and the motor. It communicates via CAN Bus or PWM. Very common on teams that use REV hardware.

VRM — Voltage Regulator Module

NI / ANDYMARK — LEGACY VOLTAGE REGULATOR

An older voltage regulation device that provided 12V and 5V regulated outputs for the radio and other small devices. Now largely replaced by the Mini Power Module and other modern solutions. You may see it on older robots or in older wiring diagrams.

Victor SPX Motor Controller

CTRE — BRUSHED MOTOR CONTROLLER (LEGACY)

An older CTRE motor controller for brushed motors (like the CIM). Communicates via CAN Bus or PWM. No built-in encoder support. Largely replaced by integrated controllers (like those in the Kraken) but still used on many active robots for mechanism motors where brushless performance isn't necessary.

Talon SRX Motor Controller

CTRE — BRUSHED MOTOR CONTROLLER WITH ENCODER SUPPORT

A step up from the Victor SPX — the Talon SRX supports external encoders (via a ribbon cable data port) and offers closed-loop PID control on the device itself. CAN or PWM. Common on robots from 2015–2022. You'll still see them at competitions powering CIM motors and other brushed mechanisms.

NEO Motor (by REV Robotics)

REV ROBOTICS — BRUSHLESS MOTOR

REV's flagship brushless motor, used with the SPARK MAX controller. Comparable in class to the older Falcon 500. Very popular on teams that standardize on REV hardware. Like the Kraken, it has a built-in encoder, but it requires a SPARK MAX controller to operate (it's not self-contained like the Kraken).

Falcon 500

CTRE — BRUSHLESS MOTOR + TALON FX (PREDECESSOR TO KRAKEN)

The predecessor to the Kraken X60. Also integrated motor + TalonFX controller, also CAN Bus. Many teams still run Falcon 500s. They're slightly heavier and lower peak power than the Kraken X60 but are proven and reliable. The wiring approach is identical to the Kraken — power from PDH, CAN from bus.

CIM Motor

VEXPRO — BRUSHED DC MOTOR (CLASSIC FRC)

The classic FRC motor for over a decade. Brushed DC, requires a separate motor controller (Talon SRX, Victor SPX, etc.). No built-in encoder. Generates more heat than brushless motors and is less efficient. Now largely replaced by brushless motors, but you'll see them everywhere on older robots.

Limelight Vision Camera

LIMELIGHT ROBOTICS — INTEGRATED VISION SYSTEM

The Limelight is a self-contained camera + processing board in one package. It connects via Ethernet to the robot's network and publishes targeting data (target angle, distance, AprilTag pose) to NetworkTables, which the roboRIO reads. An alternative to the Orange Pi 5 + PhotonVision approach. Some teams prefer it for its plug-and-play simplicity. Powers from 12V via the VRM or MPM. Addressable LEDs (WS2812B / NeoPixels)

VARIOUS — LED STRIPS

WS2812B is a type of LED strip where each LED can be individually programmed to any color. In FRC, the roboRIO has a dedicated Addressable LED port (DIO 9 / MXP) that supports WS2812B strips directly with WPILib's AddressableLED class. Common uses: displaying alliance color, game state, whether the robot has a game piece, etc. Power the strips from a 5V source (not directly from 12V) — typically a dedicated channel on the PDH through a buck converter.

Limit Switches / Magnetic Sensors

VARIOUS — DIGITAL SENSORS

Simple binary sensors — they're either ON or OFF. Limit switches are physical switches triggered by contact (like a button pushed by a mechanism reaching its travel limit). Magnetic sensors (Hall effect) detect the presence of a magnet without physical contact. Both connect to the roboRIO's DIO (Digital Input/Output) ports. Used for mechanism end stops, game piece detection, homing routines, etc.

Pneumatics (PCM/PCMH, Compressor, Solenoids)

REV / CTRE — PNEUMATIC CONTROL

Pneumatics use compressed air to actuate cylinders (like pneumatic pistons) for fast, powerful linear motion. The system includes: a compressor (pumps air, runs from the PDH), solenoids (valves that direct air to cylinders), and a PCM/PCMH (Pneumatic Control Module/Hub — manages the compressor and solenoids via CAN Bus). Pneumatics are powerful for grabbers, shooters, and any mechanism needing a fast linear push. They add weight and complexity — weigh the tradeoffs carefully. Note: pneumatics are governed by specific FRC rules about max pressure (120 PSI storage, 60 PSI working) and require a pressure relief valve.

USB Hub

VARIOUS — USB EXPANSION

A powered USB hub connected to the roboRIO's USB port allows connecting multiple USB cameras or devices. The roboRIO only has 2 USB-A ports, so a hub is needed when more USB devices are required. Use a powered hub (with its own power input from a 5V source) to prevent the roboRIO from being overwhelmed by powering multiple cameras through its USB bus.

Fuse Holders / Blade Fuses

VARIOUS — OVERCURRENT PROTECTION

Inline fuse holders are used to add overcurrent protection on individual wires outside the PDH. Blade fuses (the same type as in a car's fuse box) are the most common type in FRC. Always match fuse rating to the wire gauge — the fuse is designed to blow before the wire melts. Typical ratings: 30A for large mechanism motors, 10A for small mechanisms, 5A for sensors and lights.

Glossary

Plain-English definitions for every electrical term used on this site.

12V
12 volts — the standard operating voltage of FRC robots. Everything runs at 12V or a regulated lower voltage derived from it.
Amp / Ampere (A)
The unit of electrical current (flow). Like the flow rate of water in a pipe. More amps = more electricity flowing per second.
Autonomous (Auto)
The first 15 seconds of an FRC match where the robot runs code without driver input.
Brownout
When voltage drops below 6.8V, causing the roboRIO to disable motor outputs to protect itself. Usually caused by drawing too much current from a partially-discharged battery.
Buck Converter
A power converter that steps voltage down (e.g., 12V to 5V). Used to power devices that can't handle the robot's full 12V supply.
CANCoder
A CTRE absolute magnetic encoder that communicates over CAN Bus. Used in MK5i modules to read the swerve wheel steering angle.
CAN Bus
Controller Area Network. A two-wire communication network (yellow/green) shared by all CAN devices. Allows fast, reliable data exchange between the roboRIO and motors/sensors.
CAN ID
A unique address number (0–62) assigned to each CAN device. No two devices on the same bus can share a CAN ID.
Daisy Chain
Wiring devices in a series — from one to the next to the next — rather than each running a separate wire back to a hub. CAN Bus devices are typically daisy-chained.
DIO
Digital Input/Output. Ports on the roboRIO that read or send simple on/off signals. Used for limit switches, beam breaks, encoders, etc.
Encoder
A sensor that counts rotations (or fractions of rotations) of a shaft. Tells the code how far a motor has turned — essential for position control.
Field-Oriented Control
A swerve drive mode where "forward" always means toward the far end of the field, regardless of which way the robot is facing. Requires the Pigeon 2.0 (or other gyro) for heading.
FMS
Field Management System. The competition field's control computer that manages match timing, enables/disables robots, and connects all driver stations.
Gyroscope (Gyro)
A sensor that measures rotational rate and heading. The Pigeon 2.0 contains a 3-axis gyro. Used for navigation and field-oriented swerve.
IMU
Inertial Measurement Unit. A sensor combining gyroscope + accelerometer (and sometimes magnetometer) to track orientation in 3D. The Pigeon 2.0 is an IMU.
Motor Controller
A device that takes a command (from the roboRIO) and converts it into the right PWM/power signal to drive a motor at a desired speed and direction. In Kraken motors, this is built in (TalonFX).
NetworkTables
A distributed key-value store used in FRC to share data between the roboRIO, driver station, and vision co-processors like the Orange Pi 5. PhotonVision publishes pose data here.
Odometry
Tracking the robot's position on the field using encoder data and gyro heading. Like GPS, but calculated from the robot's own movement history.
PDH
Power Distribution Hub. The CTRE device that distributes power from the battery to all robot components through individual fused channels.
Phoenix Tuner X
CTRE's configuration and diagnostic software for all Phoenix 6 devices (Krakens, Pigeons, CANivore, etc.). Used to set CAN IDs, update firmware, and tune PIDs.
PID
Proportional-Integral-Derivative. A feedback control algorithm that calculates motor output based on the error between desired and actual state. Used for accurate position and velocity control.
Pose
The robot's position (X, Y on the field) + orientation (heading angle) combined. Used for autonomous path planning and field-oriented drive.
PWM
Pulse Width Modulation. An older way to communicate motor speed commands — a servo signal on a 3-wire cable. Still used for some sensors and legacy controllers. Less capable than CAN Bus.
RSL
Robot Signal Light. The mandatory orange indicator light that shows the robot's status (solid = disabled, blinking = enabled). FRC inspection requirement.
roboRIO
The mandatory NI robot controller for FRC. Runs robot code, manages CAN Bus, connects to radio and sensors. The "brain" of the robot.
Stall
When a motor is spinning (or trying to) but is physically blocked. The motor draws maximum current in a stall — if prolonged, it will overheat. PDH current monitoring can detect stalls.
Swerve Drive
A holonomic drivetrain where each wheel module can steer and drive independently, allowing the robot to move in any direction. Requires encoders, a gyro, and complex code.
TalonFX
CTRE's integrated motor controller, built into the Kraken X60 and X44 (and previously the Falcon 500). Communicates over CAN Bus, supports closed-loop PID control.
Teleop
The operator-controlled period of an FRC match (after autonomous). 135 seconds where the driver controls the robot via the driver station.
Termination Resistor
A 120Ω resistor at each end of a CAN Bus chain that prevents signal reflections. The PDH provides one end; the last device on the chain provides the other.
Voltage
Electrical "pressure" measured in volts (V). FRC robots run at 12V nominal.
Watt (W)
Unit of power. Watts = Volts × Amps. A Kraken X60 can peak at ~1000W. A single PWM servo might use 5W.
WPILib
The official FRC programming library, included with VS Code + FRC extensions. Provides Java/C++/Python APIs for all robot hardware and standard FRC patterns.
Yaw
Rotation around the vertical axis — turning left or right. The Pigeon 2.0's yaw reading is the robot's compass heading (0–360°).
